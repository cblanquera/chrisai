# Site Direction Playbooks

Use this reference when the design direction is still loose or when the task
needs a named visual archetype for a docs or static-site surface.

Choose one primary direction first. Borrowing accents from a second direction is
fine, but do not average several directions into a vague hybrid.

## Editorial

Use when the product needs authority, clarity, and strong reading rhythm.

- Tone: mature, thoughtful, text-forward
- Density: moderate
- Backgrounds: quiet fields with restrained texture or framing
- Typography: expressive headings, stable body text, strong contrast in scale
- Navigation: clear and understated
- Good for: guides, handbooks, design systems, thought-leadership docs
- Avoid: over-decorating code or adding marketing-style noise to article pages

## Handbook

Use when the site should feel operational, durable, and practical.

- Tone: calm, structured, reliable
- Density: medium to high
- Backgrounds: minimal, utility-first, subtle section separation
- Typography: conservative and legible with disciplined hierarchy
- Navigation: highly functional and obvious
- Good for: internal docs, operational playbooks, product handbooks
- Avoid: oversized hero treatments that misrepresent the site's utilitarian role

## Product-Doc Hybrid

Use when the homepage must both explain the product and guide users into docs.

- Tone: confident, technical, welcoming
- Density: medium
- Backgrounds: brand-forward hero with calmer downstream reading surfaces
- Typography: sharper display moments paired with restrained body typography
- Navigation: product-aware, not purely article-like
- Good for: developer tools, SDKs, platforms, AI products
- Avoid: making the homepage read like a pure marketing splash or a raw docs index

## Technical Minimal

Use when the product identity should feel precise, lean, and low-noise.

- Tone: exact, modern, stripped-down
- Density: low to medium
- Backgrounds: flat or lightly textured with deliberate spacing
- Typography: disciplined, crisp, understated
- Navigation: spare but not ambiguous
- Good for: APIs, tooling docs, minimalist products
- Avoid: sterile sameness that erases brand character

## Brand-Forward

Use when the available brand assets are strong enough to carry a more distinct
visual language.

- Tone: memorable, expressive, intentional
- Density: medium
- Backgrounds: bolder color, gradient, pattern, or shape systems
- Typography: more expressive display choices anchored by readable body text
- Navigation: still clear, but allowed more personality
- Good for: launches, signature products, visually distinct documentation sites
- Avoid: letting expression reduce legibility, especially around code or dense docs

## Direction Selection Heuristics

Prefer the direction that best fits:

- the brand signals already present in logos, UI, or screenshots
- the homepage role
- the expected ratio of reading to browsing
- whether code examples are a primary trust surface

If the direction is unclear, compare no more than three named options and pick
one recommended path.
