# Brand Voice

Use this workflow when the main problem is defining how a brand should sound
across website copy, product UI, support, social, sales, docs, or internal
communications.

This workflow produces voice and tone guidance, not page copy, content
strategy, visual identity, logo concepts, or a full brand kit.

## Ownership

This workflow owns:

- voice traits and tone spectrum
- channel-specific tone adjustments
- vocabulary, phrase, and style rules
- examples of on-brand and off-brand language
- handoff guidance for messaging and copywriting

This workflow does not own:

- positioning strategy; use `workflows/brand-strategy.md`
- full message libraries; use `workflows/brand-messaging.md`
- visual identity rules; use `workflows/brand-visual-guidelines.md`
- finished website, email, ad, or social copy

## Workflow

Work through these steps in order:

1. Check existing context.
2. Complete only the missing intake.
3. Anchor voice in strategy and audience.
4. Define voice traits.
5. Define tone by situation and channel.
6. Create language rules and examples.
7. Review for usefulness.

## Step 1: Check Existing Context

Look for existing strategy, customer language, research, website copy, product
UI text, support docs, sales material, social posts, or brand notes. Do not
assume a particular folder exists.

If strategy is missing, use `workflows/brand-strategy.md` first or produce a
provisional voice guide with assumptions labeled.

## Step 2: Intake

Capture or infer:

- audience and relationship: expert-to-beginner, peer-to-peer, advisor,
  operator, creator, institution, or community
- desired traits: direct, warm, technical, premium, playful, calm, bold,
  editorial, minimalist, reassuring, rebellious, or another direction
- channels: website, product UI, docs, support, sales, email, social, PR,
  investor, internal, or community
- boundaries: words to avoid, humor tolerance, formality, jargon, compliance,
  regional language, and accessibility needs
- examples: brands, pages, phrases, or writing samples to emulate or avoid

When asking, offer options and ask for examples where possible.

## Step 3: Anchor Voice

Tie the voice to:

- audience expectations and sophistication
- category norms and how the brand should differ
- brand strategy, values, and personality
- customer language and vocabulary
- proof points and claims the brand can support

Do not choose traits because they sound attractive. Make each trait operational.

## Step 4: Define Voice Traits

Use 3-5 traits. For each trait, define:

- what it means
- why it matters for this brand
- what it changes in writing
- what it is not
- a short example

Avoid generic trait lists without behavior. "Clear" is useful only if it says
what to cut, simplify, or prioritize.

## Step 5: Define Tone By Situation

Voice should stay stable; tone can shift by context.

Cover only relevant contexts:

- homepage and marketing pages
- product UI and microcopy
- onboarding
- support and incident communication
- technical docs
- sales and investor communication
- social/community posts
- error, apology, or high-stakes messages

For each context, define the tone, intensity, and what to avoid.

## Step 6: Create Language Rules

Include:

- words and phrases to use
- words and phrases to avoid
- grammar and punctuation preferences when relevant
- jargon policy
- humor policy
- sentence length and structure guidance
- before/after examples
- on-brand/off-brand examples

Use customer language when available. Prefer plain, specific language over
cleverness unless the strategy explicitly supports a more expressive voice.

## Step 7: Review Gate

Before treating the voice guide as complete, confirm:

- Could another writer apply it without asking for style clarification?
- Are traits distinct and behavior-based?
- Are tone shifts defined for the relevant channels?
- Are examples specific to the brand?
- Are assumptions and unsupported audience claims labeled?

Hand off to `workflows/brand-messaging.md` when the next step is message
pillars, taglines, CTAs, or boilerplate.
